Big biomedical data: the hype and reality

2018-07-11
by Paul Agapow
in science

tagged bioinformatics, academia

How useful are big data and machine learning for biomedicine?

This is a distillation of several talks I've given recently centered around the idea that machine learning in healthcare and biomedicine is massively overhyped, misunderstood and perhaps ill-suited to many of the problems that have to be solved. To be clear, I not an total sceptic. In fact I think that data-driven biology is the way of the future. But to reach that future, problems have to be recognised, admitted and tackled. There are certainly pragmatic solutions to many of the problems I list below, but that's a subject for another post.

The problems or obstacles

Big biomedical data is a lie

Despite all the talk of biomedical big data, largely it doesn't exist.

  • The average trial size on ClinicalTrials.gov is less than 100
  • The average #samples per GEO dataset < 100
  • Average GWAS cohort size ~9000 (and this is skewwed by several v. large studies, the median ~2500)
  • Real world populations will fall short ( 1,064 ICU admissions for flu in UK 2016/2017 season)

labelled data is the new oil

These fall well short of the numbers ideally required by many analytical approaches:

  • Curse of dimensionality means that we should have more samples than features
  • Deep learning requires “thousands” of samples for training (at least p^2?)
  • GWAS needs 3K+ for large effects, 10K or more for small effects ... (and maybe millions for mental illness, see recent paper)
  • Sub-populations & rare diseases will be smaller

machine learning in biomedicine: - lack of labelled datasets (of sufficient size) - sampling bias - intepretability problem - less sexy techniques work just as well - descent by graduate student - overfitting ...

Sampling bias P-hacking Garden of forking paths Reversion to mean Multiple hypothesis testing False discovery P-values Which method is best? Omnigenics (every gene effects every other gene)

Biomedical data lakes / warehouses aren’t working Each is an island unto itself Tools can’t understand data formats High demands on user (meaning, context) Poor standardisation / harmonisation tools (curation effort == analysis effort) A world of distributed data A world of many computational idioms (Self) lock-in

Compuattaiinal idioms

Data is embiggening Can’t rely on cheap computation to get us out of a hole Many HPC idioms, most awkward (e.g. Map-Reduce) Db schema struggle at scale

We constantly solve the wrong problem

Computer scientists are continually their impression of a problem which, unfortunately, is not the actual problem. This is practically every machine learning / ai in biomedicine conference. The Google flu trends project is a good example. By analysing social media, a Google team could predict how many people had flu two weeks before

(Note: the similar project for Dengue Fever seems much more useful, given that dengue is much harder to quantify and track.)

real world evidence Failure to reuse models And that. model != actionable - not everything is an image - we don't actually have biomdecial big data - if used naively, just gives you back what you told it - each model is its own kingdom

The problems with deep models

While this largely refers to deep leanring, below I'll talk about "deep models" to include other computationally intense methods that learn data to build complex models that can classify or otherwise transform data.

Deep models are not always the best choice

Usually insufficient (good labelled) data Interpretability issues

The flipside of deep learning models beging able to infer high level features in data that are not explicitly encoded by users and use these for classification is that deep learning models will use features not encoded or intended by users. For example:

  • The Google Flu trends fixated in part on reports of basketball matches, because these tended to happen at the same time of year as flu outbreaks.
  • A deep learning model trained to distinguish wolves from huskies picked up on the snow present in the background of wolve pcitures.
  • Similarly, a model trained to distinguish melanomas from harmless moles identified the ruler that was usually laid along clinical picturess of melanomoas.

And these are gross "tells", obvious tot he observer. What more subtle clues might be possible - exposure / light levels in photos, patinet outcomes shaped by hospital catchment area and thus socio-economic and hospital speiclaities

In a larger sense, models as they now stand tells you what you’ve told it

Bias towards images

Many of the early and prominent successes in deep learning have used images, for example interpreting radiological images, detecting tumours in scans, etc. But in many ways this success is deceptive. Intepreting biomedical mages is a problem that is remarkably well-formed for deep models:

  • There's already a huge prior art in image recognition that has penetrated all the way down to the consumer level (e.g. Google Image Search)
  • There's a large body of labelled data for image classification
  • Image data is already structured in a useful way for analysis

Practical issues in the use of deep learning

There are also considerable stumbling blocks for deep learning when it comes to accessing data. A growing amount of data is tied up in hospital, registry amd other secure systems, where privacy and governance issues loom large. The appropriate mechanisms and means for accessing this data are not yet in palce and hospitals are suspicious.

Furthermore, there is a trend for access of this data to be done in a federated manner - where the compuaution is taken to the data and only summary or population results are returned. We do not as yet have useful deep leanirng models that can work in this way.
